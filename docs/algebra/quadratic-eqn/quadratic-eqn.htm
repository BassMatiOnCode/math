Quadratic Equations

Formulas to solve quadratic equations

Introduction

A quadratic equation is a second-order polynomial and has the general form

ax2 + bx + c = 0

with x,a,b,c,∈ℝ, x≠0, and x is unknown.

If a was 0, the equation would degrade to a linear equation.

A quadratic equation has always two solutions x1,x2∈ℂ, with roots or zeroes on the right-hand side. The solutions may be identical, x1=x2. This is called a double root.

A quadratic equation can be factored into an equivalent form:

ax2 + bx + c = a(x−r) (x−s) = 0

with r,s as solutions or roots for x.

The quadratic formula

x1,2 = − b ± b2 − 4 a c 2a

provides the solutions fo the quadratic equation in terms of a,bandc.

The term under the square root is known as the discriminant Δ. If Δ > 0, the equation has two distinct real roots. If Δ = 0, the equation has a double real root. If Δ < 0, the equation has two complex conjugate solutions.

Geometrically, the solutions represent the points where the graph of the quadradic function y=ax2+bx+c crosses the x-axis (the x-intercepts).

Completing the Square

To derive the quadratic formula, divide the quadratic equation by a. This is allowed because a≠0. Then we subtract the third term and get

x2 + bx a = − c a

This is allowed because a≠0. Then we subtract the third term.

Now we bring the left side into the form

x2 + 2kx + k2

By comparison, we find that

2k = ba ⇔ k = b2a

Two complete the trinomial square, we add the missing k squared on both sides:

x2 + bx a + b2 4 a2 = b2 4 a2 − ca

Now we can transform the left side into a binomial square:

( x + b 2 a ) 2 = b2 4 a2 − ca

Then we introduce a common denominator on the right side:

( x + b 2 a ) 2 = b2 − 4ac 4 a2

Now we take thw square root:

x + b 2 a = ± b2 − 4ac 2 a

The ± operator is there because the square root has two possible solutions, a positive and a negative value:

The next step isolates x on left:

x = − b 2 a + ± b2 − 4ac 2 a

And we obtain the final result, the quadratic formula:

x = − b ± b2 − 4ac 2 a

Alternative Forms of the Quadratic Formula

Eliminated Denominator

We bring the denominator under the root:

x = − b 2a ± ( b 2a ) 2 − c a

This form has the advantage that we can reuse the term b/(2a), which simplifies arithmetic somewhat.

Giulio Fagnano's Form

If the roots of a quadratic equation are orders of magnitude apart, the term ac becomes very small, and the standard quadratic formula becomes unstable in numeric computations (where real numbers are approximted with floating point numbers). Then Fagnano's form should be used instead.

His form has the square root in the denominator:

x = 2c − b ± b2 − 4 a c

with c≠0.

To derive this solution, we start with the standard quatratic formula and eliminate the denominator:

2 a x = − b ± b2 − 4ac

The standard quadratic equation solved for a:

ax2 = − bx − c a = − bx + c x2

Substitute this expression for a into the equation:

−2x bx + c x2 = −2b − 2c x = − b ± b2 − 4ac

Isolate x, and you get Giulio Fagnano's equation:

x = 2c − b ± b2 − 4ac

Note that multiplication with −1 doesn't change the effect of the ± operator.
