Propositions

An alternative word for "proposition" is "statement".

Positive examples:

  • The sun shines.
  • The sky is cloudy.
  • It is cold outside.
  • The dog is 6 years old.

Negative examples:

A command is not a proposition because it cannot be true of false.

x + 1 = 2

The statement can cannot be determined without knowledge of x. Therefore, it is not a proposition.

Propositions are represented by uppercase letters. They are propositional variables. So if we designate the letters A and B to the first positive statements above, we could express that the sun shines and it is not cloudy by

Constructing Truth Tables

  • Add one column for each propsition.
  • If there are n propositions, add 2n rows.
  • Add an additional column for compound propositions (intermediate results).
  • Add the column for the final result.

The columns for the intermediate results allow to to keep computations or transformation steps simple. That helps to avoid mistakes.

Important Connectives

Negation

The NOT connective, or negation, is usually represented by a ¬, inverts a proposition:

Examples:

The sky is blue.

The sky is not blue.

It is warm outside.

It is not warm outside.

Conjunction

The AND connective, also called conjuction, is usually represented by the symbols ∧ , &, or ⋅ :

The AND connective yields true only if both connected propsitions are true. As soon as one of the propositions is false, AND yields false.

Examples:

The sky is blue and the sun is shining

Evaluates to true if both propositions are true.

Disjunction

The inclusive OR connective, or disjunktion, is usually represented by ∨ (from the latin "vel" = "or") or +:

The OR connective yields true as soon as at least one proposition is true. Conversly, the result is false only if both connected propositions are false.

Difference (XOR)

The exclusive OR (XOR) connective, or difference, usually represented by ⊕ or ⊻:

The result is true if the connected propositions are different. Conversly, the result is false if the propositions are equal.

Implication

Implication is the logical "if-then". It is also read as "implies". A is also called the hypothesis, and B is called the conclusion. Common symbols are the single right arrow → or the double right arrow ⇒.

Converse

Let the implication A⇒B. Then the converse is B⇒A.

Example: If the sun is shining, then I do go out.
Converse: If I do not go out, the sun is not shining.

Note the difference to the implication in rows 2 and 3!

Inverse

Let the implication A⇒B. Then the inverse is ¬A ⇒ ¬B.

Example: If the sun is shining, then I do go out.
Inverse: If the sun is not shining, then I don't go out.

Contrapositive

Let the implication A⇒B. Then the contrapositive is ¬B ⇒ ¬A.

Example: If the sun is shining, then I do go out.
Contrapositive: If I don't go out, the sun is not shining.

NOTE that the contrapositive truth table is identical to the implication truth table!

Tautology

A tautology is a compound proposition that is always true.

Contradiction

A contradiction is a compound proposition that is always false.

Contingency

A contingencyis a proposition which is neither a tautology nor a contradiction.
