Geometry

Overview and vocabulary

Geometry is about points, lines, planes and figures. We will concentrate on Euclidian Geometry, where parallel lines don't intersect, diverge or converge, where lines are straight and planes are "flat", and the sum of angles in every triangle is 180°.

To talk about geometry, we need a graphical language and some definition of terms:

Points are designated by uppercase letters A,B,etc. Sometimes points are numbered, such as P1,P2,P3.

The coordinates of a point are usually designated by an index letter, such as Ax,Ay,Az or P1x,P1y,P1z, with index letters for the related axis.

A vector from the origin of a coordinate system to a point A is designated by a lowercase letter a with an arrow above or a bold lowercase letter, such as a→. In the literature, vectors are often written with bold letters, such as a.

Vector components are designated with indexed lowercase letters, such as ax,ay,ay, or with numbers in a multidimensional model, such as a1,a2,a3.

Two points define a straight line going through or connecting them. The line may be unbounded (AB), or constrained on one end like a ray (AB↦), or constrained on both ends to the distance between them (AB):

The length of a line in the text or equation is indicated by vertical bars as in ❘AB❘ or |P1P2|.

Lines in drawings can also be designated with lowercase letters a,b,c, etc.

The Angle at the vertex between two rays (arms, legs, sides) are designated with greek letters, such as α,β,γ.

The orientation of the angle is usually anti-clockwise. In an SVG context, where the y-axis is pointing down, angles are counted clockwise instead, as shown above.

Angles can be designated by points, such as in ∡BAC, the the point in the middle represents the vertex.
