Euler's Formula

We know that the functions sinx, cosx and ex can be developed into Taylor series:

cosx = ∑ n=0 ∞ −1n (2n)! x 2n = 1 − x2 2! + x4 4! − x6 6! ⋯
sinx = ∑ n=0 ∞ −1n (2n+1)! x 2n+1 = x − x3 3! + x5 5! − x7 7! ⋯
ex = ∑n+0∞ xn n! =1 +x + x2 2! + x3 3! + x4 4! ⋯

If we compare these three series, it seems that the series of ex is the sum of the sinx and cosx series – but not quite. There are no alternating plus and minus operators.

But see what happens if x is changed to an imaginary number z=ix:

eix = ∑n+0∞ (ix)n n! =1 + ix − x2 2! −i x3 3! + x4 4! +i x5 5! − x6 6! ⋯

The fact that even powers of i are minus one introduces a minus sign every fourth term. That looks promising.

So let us separate the real from the imaginary terms:

eix = 1 − x2 2! + x4 4! − x6 6! + ⋯ + ix −i x3 3! +i x5 5! −i x5 5! ⋯

Then extract the imaginary unit from terms on the right side:

eix = 1 − x2 2! + x4 4! − x6 6! + ⋯ + i ( x − x3 3! + x5 5! − x5 5! ⋯ )

Now it is obvious:

eix = ∑ n=0 ∞ −1n (2n)! +i ∑ n=0 ∞ −1n (2n+1)! = cosx + isinx
