Complex Numbers

Complex numbers start with a seemingly simple equation:

x2+1=0

With some rearragements, the question is

x2=−1 (1)

In words: Which number, multiplied with itself, resolves to minus one? First answer is: None. Positive one times positive one is positive one, and negative one times negative one also is positive one.

That's correct for numbers in ℝ.

Let's look at the problem from a geometrical point of view:

If we go from zero to the right, we get to the positive numbers. If we go to the left, we get to the negative numbers. Addition of positive numbers brings us further to the right. Subtracting numbers brings us to the left. Which is the same as adding negative numbers. This essentially the natur of addition.

Then we have the multiplication. Multiplying a number by minus one is effectively a reflection on the origin (0), so we get to the same distance as the number, but at the opposite site of the origin.

To make the square root of negative numbers i=−1 possible, we will have to extend the real numbers in ℝ to the complex numbers in ℂ:

Real numbers are a subset of complex numbers, with the imaginary part being zero:

z=a+0ib=a

We also write

a=Re(z) b=Im(z)

to obtain the real and imaginary parts of a complex number.

Powers of i have interesting properties:

i1 = i i2 = −1 i3 = −i i4 = 1

If we look at multiplication by i as a rotation, it requires four rotations to complete a full circle. In contrast, multiplying by the real number −1 takes only two rotations to complete a circle.
