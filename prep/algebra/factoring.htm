Factrorizing

Some examples:

30=3⋅10=3⋅5⋅2
x2+5x+6=(x+2)(x+3)

Method 1: Greatest Common Factor

The first method is called "pulling out the greatest common factor". An example:

15+25x

15 can be factored into 3 ⋅ 5, and 25 can be factored into 5 ⋅ 5, so the greatest common factor GCF is 5.

So we can pull out the GCF and write the term as

5(3+5x)

Another example:

x2y+y2x3

We are looking for factors that are common to all terms, and take the factro with the smalles exponent. So the GCFs are x2 and y. So we will pull them out of the term:

=x2(y+y2x)
=x2y(1+yx)

The could have been done in one step if we had chosen to use a single GCF x2y instead of two individual GCFs.

Method 2: Grouping

An example:

x3+3x2+4x+12

Note that we have four terms. We will divide them in groups of two. The common factor of the first group is x and its smallest exponent is two, so the GCF is x2. To find the GCF in the second group, we factorize the numbers: 4=2⋅2 and 12=2⋅2⋅3, so the GCF is 2⋅2=4.

=x2(x+3)+4(x+3)

Note that the terms in the parentheses are identical, so we can factor them out, too:

=(x2+4)(x+3)

These terms cannot be factored further.

Method 3: Quadratic Expressions

A quadratic expression has the following form:

ax2+bx+c

When we extract the roots, we might a product of two terms like this:

(x−d) (x−e)

Here, d and e are the so-called roots. In the follwoing sections, we will show how to factorize the quadratic expression.

Example 1

Consider the following quadratic expression:

ax2+bx+c
= 10x2+11x−6

The first step is to rewrite bx=b1x+b2x

ax2+b1x+b2x+c

Then we get four terms that can be group and factorized as seen in the previous section.

Without going into the details, the conditions for b1 and b2 are

b1⋅b1=a⋅c=−60
b1+b1=b=11

So we are looking for a product that evaluates to minus 60, and a sum that evaluates to 11. We cannot apply the elimination methods for systems of linear equations, because this is not a linear system.

The method of choice is to factorize b1⋅b2=−60 step by step and hope that the sum equals 11. We expect a negative product, so one of the factors must be negative. The attempts are documented in the following table:

b1 b1 Π Σ
−1 60 −60 59
−2 30 −60 28
−3 20 −60 17
−4 15 −60 11

So we have a solution for our replacement:

bx=−4x+15x

This expression replaces the term 11x in the original equation:

10x2+11x−6
= 10x2−4x+15x−6

Now we have four terms that can be grouped and have the GCF extracted:

= 2x(5x−2) + 3(5x−2)

Then we can extract the common factor and get the desired factorization:

= (2x + 3)(5x−2)

To verify the result, we distribute and should get the original expression:

= 10x2−4x+15x−6
= 10x2−11x−6

This is the original expression, the solution is correct.

Example 2

Let's factorize the following expression:

x2−6x+8

The goal is to rewrite the term bx:

x2−b1x+b2x+8

b1 and b2 must fulfill the following equations:

b1⋅b1=a⋅c=8
b1+b1=b=−6

This is not a system of linear equations, so we cannot use the Gauss' elimination method here. The appropriate method is to factorize the eight and see if the factors add up to minus 6:

b1 b2 Π Σ
1 8 8 9
2 4 8 6
−1 −8 8 −9
−2 −4 8 −6

The last combination fulfills the requirements. So our expression can be rewritten:

x2−6x+8
= x2−2x−4x+8

We now have four terms that can be grouped and the GCFs extracted

= x(x−2)−4(x−2)

and the common factor extracted, which gives us the desired factorization:

= (x−4) (x−2)

Proof: Distribution obtains us

= x2−2x−4x+8
= x2−6x+8

Which is the original equation. So the solution is correct.

Normalized Quadradic Expressions

If we compare the results for b1=−2 and b2=−4 with the result above, we can find that we don't have to go through grouping and extraction of GCFs, because b1 and b2 are already the roots:

(x−2) (x−4)

This shortcut can always be used if the quatratic expression is normalized, i. e. the factor before x2 is 1.

Method 4: Difference of Squares

A difference of squares can be factorized, a sum on squares cannot:

a2−b2=(a+b)(a−b)

Example 1:

x2−16
=x2−42
=(x+4)(x−4)

Example 2:

9x2−1
=(3x)2−12
= (3x−1) (3x+1)

Method 5: Sum or Difference of Cubes

In contrast to a sum of squares, a sum of cubes can be factorized:

a3−b3
=(a−b)(a2+ab+b2)

The formula for a sum of is quite similar, the first two signs are different:

a3+b3
=(a+b)(a2−ab+b2)

Example:

x3+27
=x3+33
=(x+3)( x2−3x+9)
