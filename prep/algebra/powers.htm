Algebraic Operations

The algebraic operations comprise

  • addition
  • subraction
  • multiplication
  • division
  • raising to a whole number of power
  • extracting roots

These operations may be performed on numbers, in which case they are called arithmetic operations. They may also be performed on variables, arithmetic expressions, or, more generally, on algebraic structures.

Exponentiation

Consider the following series of multiplications:

a⋅a⋅a⋅a=a4

DEFINITION 1: Power of a Number

A series of n multiplications of the same operand a can be written with base a and exponent n. Read: "a to the power of 4" or "a to the 4th power" or just "a to the fourth".

a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a ⏞n times = ∏ 1 n a = an

with n∈ℤ,a≠0∨n≠0,a∈ℝ

NOTE that the term a≠0∨n≠0 means that both n and a cannot be zero at the same time; 00 is undefined!

Now we can derive a set of rules for working with powers.

Product Rule

RULE 1 : Product Rule

am⋅an=am+n

PROOF:

We begin with the product of the two powers and expand them according to definition 1:

am⋅an = a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a ⏞m times ⋅ a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a ⏞n times =

Then it is obvious that we have (m+n) factors of a:

a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a ⏞m+n times =

Which we can re-transform to a power of a according to the definition 1

=am+n

q.e.d.

Quotient Rule

The quotient rule is used if one power is divided by a power of the same variable:

RULE 2: Quotient of Powers

am an = am−n

PROOF:

We start with the left side and expand the terms according to definition 1:

am an = a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏞ m times a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ n times =

Assuming m > n, we can visually(!) split the numerator in two parts:

a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏞ m−n times ⋅ a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏞ n times a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ n times =

Obviously, the right term is equal to one and therefore disappears. The left term remains and is transformed according to definition 1:

a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏞ m−n times = am−n

Assuming m < n, we can visually split the denominator into two parts:

1 a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ n−m times ⋅ a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏞ m times a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ m times =

Again, the right term cancels out, the left term remains:

1 a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ n−m times = a−(n−m) = am−n

We get n − m factors remaining in the denominator, ands ince m is smaller than n, we expect the exponent to become negative. Therefore the minus in the exponent. If we distribute that minus into the bracket, the expected term m − n remains in accordance with the conjecture.

Remains the case m = n:

am−n = a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏞ m times a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ n times = 11 = 1

The number of factors in the numerator equals the number of factors in the denominator, so they cancel out completely to a one. Since m = n, the exponent becomes zero, and we have the following equation:

a0=1

Q.e.d.

Two special facts obtained in this proof should be promoted to their own rules. First, negative exponents:

RULE 3: Multiplicative Inverse or Reciprocal

The reciprocal of a number a, raised to the power of n, is equal to the numper a raised to the negative power −n:

a−n=1a+n , a+n=1a−n

The second fact is a number a raised to the power of zero:

RULE 4: A number a≠0 raised to the zero-th power is one:

a0=1

Power Rule

If a power is raise to a power, we can multiply the exponents:

RULE 4: Exponentiation Rule

(am)n=amn

PROOF:

Firs we will substitute b for am, which should make the equation simpler:

(am)n = (b)n = bn

This can be transformed according to definition 1 to

= b⋅b⋅ ⋯ ⋅b⋅b ⏟ n times

Then we re-substitute am for each b and obtain:

= a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ m times ⋅⋯⋅ a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ m times ⏞ n ⋅ m times

We have n groups with m factors, so there are m times n factors in total. That can be tranformed according definition 1 to

= amn

Q.e.d.

Fractional Exponents

DEFINITION 2: Rasing a number a to the fraction of n can be written as n-th root:

a1/n=a1n=an

A fractional exponent, similar to division, is the inverse of the power rule:

RULE 5: Fractional Exponent

The exponent 1 over n is the exponential inverse of the exponent n:

(an) 1/n = a n⋅1/n = a1 = a

PROOF:

We substitute b for 1/n and transorm according to definition 1:

(an) 1/n = (an) b = an⋅b = a

This can only be true if n⋅b=1. Therefore we can conclude:

b=1n

and

an⋅b = an⋅1n = a1 = a

Q.e.d.

Distributivity

An exponent is distributed over a product according to rule 6:

RULE 6: Exponent distribution over a product

(a⋅b) n = an bn

PROOF:

If we substitute c for a⋅b and then expand according to definition 1:

(a⋅b) n = cn = c⋅c⋅⋯⋅c⋅c ⏟ n times

Then we can re-substitute and order the factors, exploiting the associativity of multiplication:

(a⋅b)⋅⋯⋅(a⋅b) ⏟ n times
= a⋅a⋅⋯⋅a⋅a ⏟ n times ⋅ b⋅b⋅⋯⋅b⋅b ⏟ n times

Now we can re-transform the groups according to definition 1:

= an ⋅ bn

Q.e.d.

An exponent can also be distributed over a quotient:

RULE 7: Exponent distribution over a quotient

( ab ) n = an bn

PROOF:

We substitute c for 1/n and apply rule 6:

( ab ) n = (a⋅ c) n = an ⋅ cn

This can be transformed using definition 1:

= an ⋅ c⋅⋯⋅c ⏟ n times

Resubstituted and simplified:

= an ⋅ 1b⋅⋯⋅1b ⏟ n times = an ⋅ 1 b⋅⋯⋅b ⏟ n times

Now we can re-transform the bs in the denominator according to definition 1, which gives us the final result:

= an ⋅ 1 bn = an bn

Q.e.d.
